Part 1 · Face Morphing

One photo, two identities

A morphed passport photo can pass automated and human face verification for both people who contributed to it. That's not a hypothetical — it's a documented border‑control vulnerability.

Real‑world precedent

Ferrara, Franco & Maltoni, "The Magic Passport" (IJCB 2014) — first to demonstrate that a single morphed enrolment photo can be matched against two distinct live subjects by both humans and automated systems.

What to explain

  • This is the hook — say it plainly, don't rush it.
  • One synthetic photo → issued to person A → used at the border by person B.

Say it like this

"If this one photo can fool a border-control face match for two different passports, that's a real vulnerability — not an academic curiosity."

Part 1 · Method

Five steps from two photos to one morph

1

Detect landmarks

MediaPipe FaceLandmarker — 478‑pt mesh, +8 fixed frame/corner points so the background warps too.

2

Match correspondence

Point i on face A = point i on face B — same fixed topology, no separate registration step.

3

Average the shape

Target landmarks = (1−α)·A + α·B for the chosen blend factor.

4

Delaunay‑triangulate

~950 triangles computed once, on the averaged shape — valid for warping both faces.

5

Warp + cross‑dissolve

Per‑triangle affine warp from both faces into the target shape, blended (1−α, α).

What to explain

  • Walk left to right, one sentence per step — don't dive into the math live.
  • Call out the 8 boundary points as a deliberate engineering choice.

Say it like this

"Same 478 points on both faces means correspondence is free — point 200 on my face is always the left eye corner, so we never need registration."

Used

landmarks.pymorph.pycv2.getAffineTransform
Part 1 · Interpolation

Watching identity blend, step by step

α = 0 (Subject A) → 0.25 → 0.5 (the attack image) → 0.75 → 1 (Subject B)

Part 1 · Limitations

Where it visibly breaks

  • Mismatched expression (one smiling, one neutral) → ghosting / double‑exposure around the mouth
  • A feature present in only one subject (e.g. glasses) fades in/out rather than looking physically real
  • Small seams near the image border — background pixels are only weakly constrained by the triangulation
  • Simple cross‑dissolve, not Poisson/gradient‑domain blending — skin tone & lighting aren't colour‑corrected
  • Low source resolution (125×94 px, upscaled 3×) caps the fine detail achievable

What to explain

  • Own these before anyone asks — it reads as rigor, not weakness.

Say it like this

"Naming our own limitations up front usually heads off half the questions before they're asked."

Part 2 · Method

Color, scale, texture, then a vote

1

Color spaces

YCbCr + HSV — 6 one‑channel images per input photo.

2

Laplacian pyramid

2 scale levels of high‑frequency detail, per channel.

3

Texture features

LBP + HOG + BSIF extracted per (channel, scale), concatenated per descriptor type.

4

CRC classifier

One Collaborative Representation Classifier per feature type; scores by reconstruction residual.

5

Sum‑rule fusion

Z‑normalize the 3 scores, add them, threshold for the final decision.

What to explain

  • Keep CRC to one plain sentence — don't derive the ridge‑regression math live.

Say it like this

"CRC represents the test image as a weighted mix of training images. If it reconstructs well from the bona‑fide training set, it's probably bona fide."

Part 2 · Honesty check

What 51 test images does — and doesn't — tell you

  • Each misclassified image moves APCER/BPCER by ~2–6 percentage points — a staircase, not a smooth curve
  • BSIF alone is the clear weak link (23.5% vs. 5.9%) — our self‑learned 8‑bit filter bank underperforms the paper's tuned 12‑bit bank
  • LBP and HOG alone already match the fused ensemble — BSIF isn't currently pulling its weight
  • Read these numbers as indicative of a working pipeline, not as tight, publishable estimates

What to explain

  • This is the single most important slide for the grade — it shows statistical judgment, not just code that runs.

Say it like this

Engineering note

This course folder's path contains Hebrew characters. OpenCV's imread/imwrite and MediaPipe's native core both silently fail on non‑ASCII Windows paths. Fixed with (1) a Unicode‑safe image‑I/O wrapper routing through NumPy + cv2.imencode/imdecode, and (2) a plain NTFS directory junction — an ASCII‑only alias path — so MediaPipe's C++ core can start at all.

Anticipated questions

Q&A prep

Why CRC instead of an SVM or a small CNN?

It's the paper's own method — a closed‑form ridge‑regression solve, no iterative training, and it works fine with very limited per‑class training data.

Why is the dataset so much smaller than the paper's?

Course‑project scope — 1,309/2,608 print‑scanned images isn't sourceable in the time available. We kept the full pipeline structure and scaled down; results still land in the paper's own D‑EER range.

Isn't 51 test images too few to trust?

For tight estimates, yes — flagged directly in the report. It's enough to sanity‑check the pipeline works end‑to‑end and behaves as the literature predicts.

Would a CNN classifier beat this?

Likely, given enough data — that's the direction S‑MAD research has moved since 2020. Out of scope here; noted as future work.

  • Know the shape of each answer — don't read them verbatim off the slide.

Closing

Two‑part deliverable, one pipeline
